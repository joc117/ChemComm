Mustard Gas (Sulfur Mustard)

Chemical Warfare, first implemented in WW1 by the German army, uses the chemical properties of certain chemical agents to inflict harm and incapacitate opposing troops. One of the most notable examples of chemical warfare agents is Mustard Gas or Sulfur Mustard (Câ‚„Hâ‚ˆClâ‚‚S), which is a cellular poison, mutagen, and ironically enough a recognized human carcinogen. In addition, autopsies performed on 75 soldiers who had died of mustard agent during World War I reported decreased counts of white blood cells, which motivated scientists to further research its possible applications to treating hematologic cancers, which are characterized by their abnormal white blood cell proliferation.

Nitrogen mustards (Câ‚†Hâ‚â‚‚Clâ‚ƒN) were first synthesized in the 1930s. These compounds were modifications of sulfur mustard and were found to have greater systemic toxicity than sulfur mustard (Gilman and Philips, 1946; OSRD, 1946). Particularly potent was the effect of nitrogen mustard on cells that are actively proliferating, including the lymphoid tissue, bone marrow, and certain cells lining the gastrointestinal tract. A Yale research group successfully performed a trial on tumour ridden mice, motivating the first ever human chemotherapeutic trial.

Modern day Chemotherapeutic drugs utilise the same mechanism that were discovered to explain the cytotoxicity of Nitrogen Mustards. Nitrogen Mustards serve as alkylating agents, meaning that they can donate alkyl groups to nucleophilic sites on DNA bases, most commonly guanine at the N7 position, as it is highly reactive. The Chemo drugs form aziridinium ring which has a high reactivity due to their three membered ring.The N7 nitrogen in guanine has a lone pair, making it a strong nucleophile. This allows for DNA alkylation where the alkyl groups are added to the DNA strands, causing cross linking, preventing replication and causing apoptosis.

Modern day chemotherapeutic drugs build on these principles of alkylation and cross-linking, such as cyclophosphamide which is used to treat for lymphomas and leukemia, and Nitrosoureas which are lipophilic alkylating agents, allowing them to treat for brain tumours like glioblastoma.

Nitrogen Mustards

Nitrogen mustards undergo intramolecular cyclisation to form aziridinium ions, which react with nucleophilic guanine at N7. Since the Aziridinium ion has two electrophilic sites, it will react with another base pair in the DNA strand, thus prohibiting mitosis. However, they are not selective to cancer cells, which restricts their clinical use.

Nitrosoureas

Nitrosoureas contain a nitroso functional group (-N=O) that decomposes to form isocyanate and chloroethyl compounds. The chloroethyl compound forms a carbocation that causes DNA alkylation at the N7 guanine site. Isocyanate inhibits DNA repair enzymes through carbamylation, leading to apoptosis. Their high lipophilicity allows them to pass through the Blood-Brain barrier, making them effective for brain tumors.

Alkyl Sulfonates

Methanesulfonates form carbocations when the mesylate group leaves. With two mesylate functional groups, there are two electrophilic sites. One site attaches to the nucleophilic N7 guanine (monoalkylation), while the other attaches to other nucleotide base pairs (bis-alkylation), forming either interstrand (N7-G:N7-G) or intrastrand (N7-G:N3-A) crosslinks.

Oxazaphosphorines

These alkylating agents are characterized by a phosphorus-nitrogen bond that prevents direct ionization of the bis(2-chloroethyl) moiety. They are pro-drugs activated by liver cytochromes (P450). The oxidation of carbon 4 of the heterocycle allows the formation of chloroethylaziridine responsible for cross-linking. Used in combination with other agents for sarcoma and solid tumors.

Ethyl Imines and Polyaziridines

Characterized by one or more aziridine cycles, these compounds alkylate DNA similarly to nitrogen mustards but with less reactive uncharged aziridine cycles. Used for ovarian, breast, and bladder cancers.

Mitomycin C

An antibiotic with two aziridine cycles and a quinone moiety. Its alkylating mechanism involves activation by enzymatic reduction, leading to protonation and opening of the aziridine cycle. Used for various adenocarcinomas.

Triazenes and Hydrazines

Precursors of methyldiazonium ion responsible for alkylation. DNA methylation occurs on adenine N3 and guanine N7 and O6, with O6-methyl guanine being particularly cytotoxic. Used for melanomas, lymphomas, and brain tumors.
